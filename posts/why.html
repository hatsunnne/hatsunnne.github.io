why i wanted to join web3

#web3 #journey #personal

Honestly, it started with boredom. I had nothing going on — no direction, no project, just endless hours scrolling through the internet looking for something that would actually make me feel alive. Then one day I stumbled into a rabbit hole about smart contracts, about code that runs on a blockchain with no middleman, no permission needed, no one to stop it. Code that holds millions of dollars and lives entirely on its own. I thought to myself: this is either the most beautiful or the most terrifying thing I've ever seen. Probably both. And that's exactly what hooked me.

Code that holds millions of dollars and lives entirely on its own — this is either the most beautiful or the most terrifying thing I've ever seen.

I genuinely believe Web3 is the future of the internet. Not the hype, not the speculation, not the noise on Twitter — but the actual technology underneath. The idea that you can build systems where trust is replaced by math, where transparency isn't optional but built into every transaction, where anyone with a laptop can verify what's happening without asking for permission. That's not just a cool technology — that's a fundamental shift in how humans coordinate. And I want to be part of it, even if I'm 16 and still have a lot to learn.

The thing about Web3 that really got me is the security side. When I realized that a single bug in a smart contract can drain millions in seconds — and that auditors are the last line of defense between a protocol and catastrophe — I knew that's where I wanted to be. Not building the next token or the next NFT marketplace, but sitting in the shadows reading bytecode, finding the vulnerability that nobody else saw, and making sure the protocol survives. There's something poetic about it: breaking things to protect them.

I'm not going to pretend I know everything. I'm 16, I'm still learning Solidity, still wrapping my head around the EVM, still getting confused by storage layouts and delegate calls. But that's the point — I love the fact that there's always something deeper to understand, always another exploit to study, always another protocol to dissect. This space doesn't wait for you to be ready. It moves fast, it breaks things, and it rewards the curious. And if there's one thing I know about myself, it's that I'll never stop being curious about how things break.

— hatsunnne
